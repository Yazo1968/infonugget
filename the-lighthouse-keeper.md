# The Lighthouse Keeper of Starfish Bay

## Chapter 1: The Letter in the Bottle

### The Bottle in the Rocks

On the morning of her tenth birthday, Mira Sandoval found a glass bottle wedged between the rocks at the bottom of the cliff path. Inside was a letter written in deep blue ink on paper that smelled like salt and old libraries.

*Dear Whoever Finds This,*

*The lighthouse at Starfish Bay needs a new keeper. The last one — that would be me — has grown too old to climb the 142 steps each evening. The light must not go dark. If it does, the ships will lose their way, and the starfish will forget how to glow.*

*If you are brave, if you are curious, and if you do not mind talking to seagulls, please come at once.*

*Yours hopefully,*
*Octavius Bloom, Lighthouse Keeper (Retired, effective immediately)*

### The Lonely Tower

Mira read the letter three times. She lived in the small coastal village of Penrose Point, where the houses were painted in cheerful colours and the wind always tasted like seaweed. She had seen the lighthouse from her bedroom window every night of her life — a tall white tower standing on the rocky headland across the bay, its beam sweeping steadily across the dark water.

But she had never been inside it. Nobody in the village had, as far as she knew. Old Mr. Bloom kept to himself, and the path to the lighthouse was steep and tangled with blackberry bushes that seemed to grow back overnight no matter how often anyone cut them.

Mira tucked the bottle into her coat pocket and looked out across the water. The lighthouse stood against the grey morning sky, and for the first time she noticed that its light was off. It was daytime, of course, so that was perfectly normal. But something about the dark windows made the tower look lonely, like a person standing at a bus stop long after the last bus has gone.

### Setting Off

"I suppose," Mira said to herself, "that I had better go and see about this."

She told her mother she was going exploring, which was true. She packed a cheese sandwich, an apple, her sketchbook, and a torch, which she thought might be useful in a lighthouse. Then she set off along the cliff path toward Starfish Bay.

## Chapter 2: The 142 Steps

### The Red Door

The path to the lighthouse was exactly as difficult as Mira had expected. The blackberry bushes grabbed at her coat and the ground was uneven with loose stones that skittered away under her boots. Twice she had to climb over fallen tree trunks, and once she disturbed a family of rabbits who looked at her with great disapproval before hopping into their burrow.

After forty minutes of walking, scrambling, and occasionally sliding, she reached the lighthouse door. It was painted red, though the paint was peeling in long strips, and there was a brass knocker shaped like a dolphin. Mira knocked three times.

The door opened almost immediately, as if someone had been standing just behind it waiting. Octavius Bloom was very tall and very thin, with a white beard that reached the third button of his waistcoat and eyes the colour of sea glass. He wore a navy blue captain's coat with brass buttons and carpet slippers that had been patched so many times they were more patch than slipper.

"Ah," he said, peering down at her. "You found the bottle."

"I did," said Mira. "I'm Mira Sandoval. I live in Penrose Point."

"I know who you are," said Mr. Bloom. "I've watched you drawing on the rocks through my telescope. You draw the sea very well for someone your age. Come in, come in. Mind the cat."

### Admiral Nelson

The cat in question was an enormous ginger tom who was lying across the doorway like a furry draught excluder. He opened one eye as Mira stepped over him, decided she was not interesting, and went back to sleep.

"That's Admiral Nelson," said Mr. Bloom. "He is useless as a mouser but excellent company during storms."

Inside, the lighthouse was round, as lighthouses are, and every wall was covered with shelves. The shelves held books, jars of sea glass sorted by colour, coils of rope, navigational instruments made of brass, old photographs in silver frames, and at least a dozen model ships in bottles. A spiral staircase made of iron wound upward through the centre of the room, disappearing into the ceiling.

"One hundred and forty-two steps to the lamp room," said Mr. Bloom, following her gaze. "I counted them every day for forty-seven years. My knees have formally resigned from the task."

### The Keeper's Duties

He made tea in a blue enamel pot and poured it into cups that did not match. They sat at a table by the window, and he told her what a lighthouse keeper does.

"You must light the lamp every evening at sunset," he said. "Not a minute late. You must keep the lens clean — it is a Fresnel lens, very beautiful, very old, and it magnifies the light so that ships can see it twenty miles away. You must keep a weather log. You must sound the fog horn when visibility drops below one mile. And you must talk to the seagulls."

"Why must I talk to the seagulls?" asked Mira.

"Because if you don't," said Mr. Bloom very seriously, "you will go mad from the quiet."

## Chapter 3: The Fresnel Lens

### The Glass Beehive

Mira climbed the 142 steps for the first time that afternoon. By step eighty she was breathing hard. By step one hundred and ten her legs were trembling. By step one hundred and thirty she was considering whether it would be acceptable for a lighthouse keeper to simply live at the top and never come down.

But when she reached the lamp room, she forgot about her legs entirely.

The Fresnel lens was the most beautiful thing she had ever seen. It stood in the centre of the room like a giant glass beehive, nearly as tall as Mira herself, made of hundreds of precisely cut glass prisms arranged in concentric rings. Each prism caught the afternoon light and split it into tiny rainbows that danced across the curved walls. At the heart of the lens sat the lamp — a modern electric bulb now, Mr. Bloom explained, though it had once burned whale oil, then paraffin, then acetylene gas.

"The lens was made in Paris in 1867," said Mr. Bloom, who had followed her up at a pace that suggested his knees were not quite as retired as he claimed. "A man named Augustin-Jean Fresnel invented this design. Before his lenses, ships could only see a lighthouse from a few miles away. His invention saved thousands of lives."

### How Light Bends

Mira reached out and touched one of the glass prisms. It was cool and smooth, and up close she could see that it was not perfectly clear — there were tiny bubbles trapped inside the glass, frozen in place for over a hundred and fifty years.

"How does it work?" she asked.

Mr. Bloom picked up a small torch and held it at the centre of the lens. Immediately, the scattered afternoon light focused into a single brilliant beam that shot out through the lamp room windows and across the bay. Even in daylight, it was startlingly bright.

"Each prism bends the light inward," he explained. "The ones at the top and bottom catch light that would otherwise be wasted — shining up at the sky or down at the floor — and redirect it outward, horizontally. The result is that nearly all of the lamp's light is concentrated into one powerful beam. A small light becomes visible for miles and miles."

"It's like a team," said Mira. "Each piece is small, but they all work together."

### Cleaning the Prisms

Mr. Bloom smiled. "That is exactly right. And that is why each prism must be kept perfectly clean. Even a small smudge or a film of salt spray can scatter the light and weaken the beam. I clean them every morning with vinegar and a soft cloth. It takes about two hours."

He handed her the cloth and the bottle of vinegar. "Your turn."

Mira cleaned the lens for the first time that afternoon. It was painstaking work — each prism had to be wiped gently in one direction, never in circles, to avoid leaving streaks. There were two hundred and twelve prisms in total. Her arms ached by the time she finished, but when she stepped back and looked at the lens gleaming in the late afternoon light, she felt a satisfaction she had never felt before.

She had made something important ready for its job.

## Chapter 4: The First Night

### Lighting the Lamp

Sunset that evening was at 6:47 PM. Mira knew this because Mr. Bloom had given her a tide and sunrise chart for the entire year — a large, laminated sheet covered in tiny numbers that she pinned to the wall of the lamp room.

At 6:45 she climbed the stairs. At 6:46 she placed her hand on the switch. At exactly 6:47, as the last sliver of orange sun touched the horizon, she turned on the light.

The effect was immediate and magnificent. The Fresnel lens caught the electric light and transformed it into a beam so bright that Mira had to shield her eyes. The beam swept out through the windows — the lamp room sat on a rotating platform that turned slowly, driven by a clockwork mechanism that Mr. Bloom wound each evening — and painted a stripe of white light across the dark water of the bay.

Mira watched the beam make its first full rotation. It took exactly fifteen seconds. Sweep, darkness, sweep, darkness. Each lighthouse had its own pattern, Mr. Bloom had told her, so that sailors could identify which lighthouse they were seeing. Starfish Bay's pattern was one flash every fifteen seconds.

### The View from Above

She pressed her face against the glass and looked out. The village of Penrose Point was a cluster of warm yellow lights on the opposite shore. She could see the harbour with its fishing boats tied up for the night, the church steeple, and the winding road that led up the hill to the school. From up here, the whole village looked like a model — small and perfect and fragile.

Below the lighthouse, the rocks of the headland dropped sharply into black water. She could hear the waves even through the thick glass, a constant low roar punctuated by the hiss of spray. It was the same sound she heard from her bedroom at home, but up here it felt closer, more alive, as if the sea were breathing.

Mr. Bloom had gone home to his cottage half a mile along the coast, leaving her alone in the lighthouse for the first time. "The light needs company," he had said. "Stay until midnight, then go home and sleep. I'll watch it till dawn — my knees can manage the flat walk from my cottage even if they can't manage the stairs."

### Something in the Water

So Mira sat in the lamp room with her sketchbook and drew. She drew the beam cutting through the darkness. She drew the distant lights of a cargo ship moving slowly along the horizon. She drew Admiral Nelson, who had followed her up the stairs and was now asleep on the landing below, his ginger tail hanging through the railing.

At eleven o'clock, something happened that she did not draw because she was not sure she had really seen it. Out in the bay, in the shallow water near the rocks, something glowed. Not the reflected beam of the lighthouse — this was a softer light, blue-green and shimmering, moving in slow patterns across the dark water. It lasted for about thirty seconds, then faded.

Mira stared at the spot for a long time, but it did not come back.

She remembered what Mr. Bloom's letter had said: *The starfish will forget how to glow.*

"Surely not," she said to Admiral Nelson, who did not reply.

## Chapter 5: The Glowing Starfish

### The Rock Pools

The next morning, Mira went down to the rocks at low tide to investigate. The base of the lighthouse headland was a jumble of dark volcanic rock covered in barnacles, mussels, and thick green seaweed that popped underfoot. Rock pools sat in every hollow, each one a miniature world of anemones, tiny crabs, and translucent shrimp that darted away from her shadow.

In the third pool she checked, she found the starfish.

It was about the size of her hand, with five broad arms tapering to blunt points. Its skin was a deep purple, textured with tiny bumps, and it was clinging to the underside of a rock ledge just below the waterline. It looked like a perfectly ordinary starfish.

### The Blue-Green Glow

Then a cloud passed over the sun, and the pool fell into shadow. And the starfish began to glow.

The light was faint but unmistakable — a soft blue-green luminescence that pulsed gently along the edges of each arm, like a heartbeat made visible. Mira knelt on the wet rock and stared. She had heard of bioluminescence — her teacher, Ms. Okafor, had shown them a video of glowing plankton in tropical waters — but she had never heard of glowing starfish, certainly not in the cold waters of Penrose Point.

She found three more in nearby pools, all the same deep purple, all glowing faintly in the shadows. They were beautiful.

### The Lantern Stars

Back at the lighthouse that afternoon, she told Mr. Bloom what she had found. He nodded slowly and poured more tea.

"They've been here as long as I have," he said. "Longer, I expect. The old fishermen called them lantern stars. They said the starfish glow brighter when the lighthouse is lit — that the light wakes something up in them. I don't know if that's true in any scientific sense, but I've watched them for forty-seven years, and I can tell you this: on the two occasions when the light went out — once during the great storm of 1989, and once when the bulb blew in 2003 and I didn't have a spare — the starfish stopped glowing. They didn't start again until the light came back."

"Has anyone studied them?" asked Mira. "A marine biologist or someone?"

Mr. Bloom shook his head. "I wrote to the university once, years ago. They sent a very polite letter back saying that bioluminescent starfish were not known to exist in British waters and suggesting that I might be observing reflected moonlight. I wrote back and told them I could tell the difference between a glowing animal and the moon, thank you very much. They did not reply."

"We should try again," said Mira. "We should document them properly. Take photographs, measure the light, record when they glow and when they don't. If we have real data, they'll have to listen."

Mr. Bloom looked at her over his teacup with an expression she could not quite read. "You know," he said, "I think I chose my replacement rather well."

## Chapter 6: The Storm

### The Barometer Falls

The autumn weather turned cruel in the second week of October. Mira checked the barometer in the lighthouse kitchen — Mr. Bloom had taught her to read it — and watched the needle drop steadily for three days. On Thursday morning, the coastguard issued a severe weather warning: Storm Helena, moving in from the Atlantic, expected to bring winds of seventy miles per hour and heavy rain through the night.

Mira made her preparations. She checked the emergency generator in the basement of the lighthouse — a diesel machine that would keep the light running if the main power failed. She filled the paraffin lamps that served as backup to the backup. She taped the windows in the lower rooms and moved Mr. Bloom's model ships to the floor where they couldn't fall.

### Extra Sandwiches

Mr. Bloom called her on the old landline telephone that still worked in the lighthouse. "Don't stay tonight," he said. "It's too dangerous. The path will be impassable."

"The light has to stay on," said Mira.

"I know," he said. "But you are ten years old and that is a category two storm."

"I'll bring extra sandwiches," said Mira.

There was a long pause. "Bring the thermos too," said Mr. Bloom. "The big one."

### Helena Arrives

The storm arrived at four in the afternoon, two hours before sunset. Mira was already in the lighthouse with Admiral Nelson, who had refused to leave and was wedged under the kitchen table with his ears flat. The wind started as a low moan and built steadily to a howl that made the thick stone walls vibrate. Rain hit the windows horizontally, so hard it sounded like gravel being thrown.

At 5:12 PM, the power went out.

Mira had practised this. She went to the basement, checked the fuel level on the generator, and pulled the starter cord. It caught on the third pull, coughing to life with a reassuring rumble. The lights flickered back on. She climbed the 142 steps — the wind made the staircase sway slightly, which she tried not to think about — and checked the lamp. It was burning steadily.

### The Starfish Blaze

At 8:30 PM, a wave hit the base of the lighthouse hard enough to shake the building. Water sprayed up past the lamp room windows — sixty feet above sea level. Mira gripped the railing and watched the beam cut through sheets of rain and spray. Somewhere out there, she knew, there might be ships depending on that light.

At 9:15 PM, through a momentary gap in the rain, she saw them.

The starfish were glowing. Not their usual faint blue-green shimmer, but a fierce, bright light — dozens of them, maybe hundreds, spread across the rocks below the lighthouse. They pulsed in unison, like a single organism, their light visible even through the storm spray.

Mira had never seen anything like it. She grabbed her sketchbook and drew as fast as she could, trying to capture the pattern of the light before the rain closed in again.

The storm blew itself out by dawn. Mira emerged from the lighthouse into a world that looked scrubbed raw — the air so clean it almost hurt to breathe, the sea still rough but calming, the sky a pale, exhausted grey. The path back to the village was blocked by a fallen tree and she had to climb through wet branches to get home.

Her mother was waiting at the door, wrapped in a blanket, eyes red. She did not say anything. She just pulled Mira into a hug that lasted a very long time.

## Chapter 7: The Scientist

### Dr. Tanaka Arrives

Three weeks after the storm, a woman arrived in Penrose Point driving a mud-spattered Land Rover packed with equipment. Her name was Dr. Keiko Tanaka, and she was a marine biologist from the University of Plymouth. She had seen Mira's photographs.

Mira had taken Mr. Bloom's advice and done things properly. She had photographed the starfish with her mother's camera, recording the date, time, tide level, water temperature, and weather conditions for each observation. She had made a map of every rock pool where she had found glowing starfish, marking each one with a numbered flag. She had kept a log of the lighthouse beam's operation alongside her starfish observations, noting any correlation between the light being on and the starfish glowing.

She had sent all of this — forty-seven photographs, six pages of data tables, and a three-page summary — to the marine biology department at the University of Plymouth. This time, they did not send a polite dismissal.

### Examining the Specimens

Dr. Tanaka was small and energetic, with short black hair and round glasses that were always slightly askew. She wore rubber boots that were too big for her and carried a waterproof notebook in which she wrote constantly.

"These are extraordinary," she said, crouching over a rock pool where three starfish clung to the rocks. It was a cloudy morning, and they were glowing faintly. "The species appears to be *Asterias rubens* — the common starfish — but the bioluminescence is completely undocumented. This could be a new subspecies, or it could be an environmental response we've never observed before."

She took water samples, measured the light output of the starfish with a device called a spectrophotometer, and collected a tiny tissue sample from one starfish for DNA analysis. She was careful and gentle, and she talked to the starfish as she worked, apologising for the inconvenience.

### The Blue-Green Frequency

"The light spectrum is fascinating," she told Mira, showing her the readout on her laptop. "The peak wavelength is 497 nanometres — that's blue-green, right at the boundary of what the human eye is most sensitive to. It's the same wavelength that travels farthest through seawater. If these starfish are using bioluminescence to communicate with each other, they've evolved to use the most efficient frequency possible."

"Mr. Bloom thinks they respond to the lighthouse," said Mira. "He says they glow brighter when the light is on."

Dr. Tanaka looked sceptical but professional. "That would be very unusual. Bioluminescence in marine invertebrates is typically triggered by mechanical stimulation — touch or water movement — or by chemical signals. A response to an artificial light source would be... well, it would be a new discovery."

"Can we test it?" asked Mira.

Dr. Tanaka smiled. "That's exactly what I was going to suggest."

## Chapter 8: The Experiment

### Designing the Test

They designed the experiment together — Mira, Dr. Tanaka, and Mr. Bloom, sitting around the lighthouse kitchen table with Admiral Nelson draped across Dr. Tanaka's equipment case.

The plan was simple. For two weeks, they would observe the starfish under four conditions: lighthouse on with observers present, lighthouse on without observers, lighthouse off with observers present, and lighthouse off without observers. For the unobserved conditions, Dr. Tanaka set up underwater cameras with light sensors that could record the starfish continuously.

"We need to control for other variables," said Dr. Tanaka. "Moon phase, tide level, water temperature, wave action, time of day, weather conditions. The more factors we account for, the stronger our conclusions will be."

### Forty-Seven Years of Data

Mira made a data collection sheet with columns for every variable. Mr. Bloom contributed his forty-seven years of weather logs — neat handwritten entries in a series of leather-bound notebooks that filled an entire shelf.

"These are remarkable," said Dr. Tanaka, turning the pages carefully. "You've been keeping daily records since 1977?"

"A lighthouse keeper keeps records," said Mr. Bloom. "It's part of the job."

The experiment began on a Monday. Each evening, Mira climbed the 142 steps, either to turn the light on or to leave it off according to the schedule. On the "off" evenings, she placed a temporary navigation light — a battery-powered lantern — on the rocks below to ensure ships were still warned of the headland. Dr. Tanaka had arranged this with the harbour authority, who were not entirely happy about it but agreed to a two-week trial.

### The Vibration Hypothesis

By the end of the first week, the pattern was clear.

The starfish glowed more intensely on nights when the lighthouse was lit. Not a little more — significantly more. Dr. Tanaka's sensors measured an average bioluminescent output 340% higher on lighthouse-on nights compared to lighthouse-off nights. The effect was consistent across different moon phases and weather conditions.

"I don't understand the mechanism," said Dr. Tanaka, staring at her data with the expression of someone who has just been told that the law of gravity takes weekends off. "There's no known photoreceptor in *Asterias* species that would respond to a light source at this distance. And yet the data is unambiguous."

"Maybe they're not responding to the light directly," said Mira. She had been thinking about this while cleaning the Fresnel lens that morning. "Maybe they're responding to the vibration. The lamp room mechanism makes a low hum when it's running — I can feel it through the floor. Sound travels well through water. Maybe the starfish can feel the vibration through the rocks and the water, and that's what triggers their glow."

Dr. Tanaka stared at her. Then she pulled out her notebook and began writing very fast. "That's... actually a testable hypothesis. Mechanical vibration as a trigger for bioluminescence. We'd need a hydrophone to measure the acoustic output of the lighthouse mechanism, and compare it with the bioluminescent response curves..."

She looked up. "Mira, do you want to be a scientist when you grow up?"

"I want to be a lighthouse keeper," said Mira. "But I think a lighthouse keeper should also be a scientist."

## Chapter 9: The Council Meeting

### The Letter

The trouble started on a Tuesday in November. Mira's mother showed her the letter over breakfast — it was from the Penrose Point Town Council, and it was addressed to Mr. Octavius Bloom.

The council had decided that the Starfish Bay lighthouse was no longer necessary. Modern ships used GPS and electronic chart systems. The lighthouse was expensive to maintain. The land it stood on had been valued at a considerable sum by a property developer who wanted to build holiday cottages. The council would be voting on the decommissioning of the lighthouse at their next meeting, in three weeks' time.

"They can't," said Mira.

"They can, sweetheart," said her mother. "The council owns the lighthouse."

### Forty-Seven Years

Mira went to see Mr. Bloom. She found him in his cottage, sitting in his armchair with Admiral Nelson on his lap and the council's letter on the table beside him. He looked very old and very tired.

"Forty-seven years," he said quietly. "I always knew this day would come. GPS is very clever. It doesn't need a man with bad knees climbing one hundred and forty-two steps."

"But the starfish," said Mira. "If the lighthouse goes dark—"

"The starfish will stop glowing," said Mr. Bloom. "Yes."

"Then we have to stop them. The council. We have to show them why the lighthouse matters."

Mr. Bloom looked at her for a long time. "And how do you propose we do that?"

### Building the Case

Mira did not answer immediately. She went home and sat on her bed and thought very hard for a very long time. Then she called Dr. Tanaka.

Over the next three weeks, Mira organised the most ambitious project of her ten-year life. She and Dr. Tanaka wrote a scientific report on the bioluminescent starfish, complete with data, photographs, and Dr. Tanaka's preliminary genetic analysis, which had confirmed that the Starfish Bay population was indeed genetically distinct — a unique variant found nowhere else in the world. Dr. Tanaka submitted the paper to a scientific journal.

Mira visited every house in Penrose Point and talked to the fishermen, the shopkeepers, the retired couples, and the families with children. She asked them what the lighthouse meant to them. She wrote down their answers in her sketchbook.

### Voices of the Village

Harbour master Jim Bates said: "My grandfather sailed by that light. My father sailed by that light. I won't see it go dark."

Eleanor Pascoe, who was ninety-three years old, said: "I was a little girl during the war. When everything was blacked out and frightening, the one thing we could count on was the lighthouse. They kept it going all through the war. It meant someone was watching out for us."

Tommy Trevayne, who was seven, said: "It's the best thing in the whole village. If they turn it off, what will I look at when I can't sleep?"

Mira put all of this together into a presentation. She practised it in front of Admiral Nelson, who was a demanding audience because he fell asleep halfway through and she had to work out which parts were boring.

## Chapter 10: The Vote

### The Developer's Pitch

The council meeting was held in the village hall on a wet Wednesday evening. Mira had never been to a council meeting before. The room was arranged with folding chairs facing a long table where the five council members sat. The mayor, Mrs. Patricia Hambly, was a large woman with a loud voice and a habit of tapping her pen on the table when she was impatient.

The property developer, Mr. Gerald Fisk, spoke first. He was a man in an expensive suit who smiled a great deal and used words like "opportunity" and "economic growth" and "luxury coastal living." He showed computer-generated images of the holiday cottages he wanted to build — modern glass-and-timber structures that looked nothing like anything in Penrose Point. He talked about jobs and tourism revenue and rates income for the council.

### Mira's Stand

Then it was Mira's turn.

She stood up, and for a moment her voice didn't work. The room was full of adults looking at her, and the microphone made a horrible squeal when she got too close to it. She stepped back, took a breath, and began.

She didn't talk about economics or GPS or property values. She talked about the starfish. She showed Dr. Tanaka's photographs — the blue-green glow against dark rocks, the pulsing patterns during the storm, the detailed close-ups showing the unique genetic markers. She explained what a Fresnel lens was and how it worked, and why the lighthouse had been important for over a hundred and fifty years.

Then she read the words of the people she had talked to. Jim Bates and Eleanor Pascoe and Tommy Trevayne and twenty-seven others. She read their words simply and clearly, without adding anything of her own.

### Four to One

Finally, she said: "The lighthouse is not just a building. It is not just a navigation aid. It is a living part of this community and a home to a species that exists nowhere else on Earth. If we let the light go dark, we lose something that we can never get back. And I don't think we should make that choice just because someone in a nice suit tells us we'll make money from it."

There was a silence. Then Jim Bates started clapping. Then Eleanor Pascoe. Then Tommy Trevayne, loudly and enthusiastically. Then the entire room.

Mrs. Hambly tapped her pen on the table for quiet.

The vote was four to one in favour of keeping the lighthouse.

## Chapter 11: The Discovery

### The Published Paper

Dr. Tanaka's paper was published in the journal *Marine Biology* the following spring. It was titled "Bioluminescent Response to Mechanical Vibration in a Genetically Distinct Population of *Asterias rubens* from Southwest England." It confirmed Mira's hypothesis: the starfish were responding to the low-frequency vibrations produced by the lighthouse's rotating mechanism, transmitted through the bedrock and the water column to the rock pools below.

The paper caused a sensation in the marine biology world. Scientists from four countries contacted Dr. Tanaka to request permission to study the Starfish Bay population. A documentary crew from the BBC came to film the glowing starfish for a nature programme. The segment, narrated by a famous naturalist, was watched by three million people.

### Lantern Star Fever

Penrose Point suddenly had more visitors than it knew what to do with. The café on the harbour started selling "Lantern Star Soup" (it was just fish chowder, but the name was popular). The gift shop stocked plush glowing starfish toys. Jim Bates started running boat tours past the lighthouse at dusk so visitors could see the glow from the water.

Through all of this, Mira kept doing her job. Every evening at sunset, she climbed the 142 steps and turned on the light. Every morning, she cleaned the 212 prisms of the Fresnel lens with vinegar and a soft cloth. She kept the weather log, sounded the fog horn when visibility dropped, and talked to the seagulls, who turned out to be surprisingly good listeners once you got used to their tendency to interrupt.

### Constance Bell

Mr. Bloom came to the lighthouse most afternoons. His knees would no longer let him climb to the lamp room, but he sat in the kitchen with Admiral Nelson and told Mira stories about the lighthouse's history — the shipwrecks it had prevented, the storms it had weathered, the keepers who had tended the light before him.

"My predecessor was a woman named Constance Bell," he told her one afternoon. "She kept this light for thirty-one years, from 1930 to 1961. During the war, she maintained the light alone for six years while every able-bodied man in the village was away. She never missed a single sunset."

"Is she the person Eleanor Pascoe was talking about?" asked Mira. "The light that was kept going all through the war?"

"The very same. Constance would have liked you, Mira. She believed that small things done faithfully mattered more than grand gestures done once."

### Small Things Done Faithfully

Mira thought about this as she climbed the stairs that evening. She thought about Constance Bell climbing these same steps during the blackout, keeping the light burning while the world was at war. She thought about Mr. Bloom climbing them for forty-seven years, day after day, caring for a lens made in Paris in 1867. She thought about the starfish in their rock pools, glowing in response to vibrations that had been humming through the headland for over a century.

Small things done faithfully.

She turned on the light.

## Chapter 12: The New Keeper

### Anniversary at Low Tide

On the first anniversary of the day she found the bottle, Mira went down to the rocks at low tide. It was early morning, the sun just clearing the hills behind the village, and the rock pools were still in shadow. The starfish were glowing — five of them in the pool closest to the lighthouse base, their blue-green light pulsing in the slow, steady rhythm she had come to know as well as her own heartbeat.

She sat on the rocks and opened her sketchbook to a fresh page. Over the past year, her drawings had filled three complete books. She had drawn the lighthouse in every kind of weather — in summer sunshine and winter gales, in fog so thick the beam was just a bright smudge, in clear cold nights when the stars were as sharp as the prisms in the Fresnel lens. She had drawn the starfish in all their moods — the faint shimmer of calm nights, the fierce blaze of storms, the gentle pulse of ordinary evenings.

### The Artist's Eye

She had also drawn other things. She had drawn Dr. Tanaka crouching over a rock pool with her spectrophotometer. She had drawn Mr. Bloom asleep in his armchair with Admiral Nelson on his chest. She had drawn the Fresnel lens with the sunset behind it, each prism a tiny fire.

Her drawings had been exhibited at the village hall, and then at the Plymouth Marine Aquarium, and then at a gallery in London that specialised in natural history art. A publisher had contacted her mother about a book. "When I'm ready," Mira had said, which she thought was a very grown-up thing to say.

### A Letter in a Bottle

But this morning she was not drawing. She was writing a letter.

She wrote it on good paper, in blue ink, and she rolled it carefully and slipped it into a glass bottle she had saved from the kitchen — a pickle jar, cleaned and dried, which was not as romantic as whatever Mr. Bloom had used but would keep the water out just as well.

The letter said:

*Dear Whoever Finds This,*

*The lighthouse at Starfish Bay has a keeper. She is eleven years old, and she is not going anywhere. But one day — many years from now — she will need someone to carry on.*

*If you are brave, if you are curious, and if you do not mind talking to seagulls (or glowing starfish), please keep this letter safe. Your turn will come.*

*Yours faithfully,*
*Mira Sandoval, Lighthouse Keeper*

She sealed the bottle with candle wax, as Mr. Bloom had shown her, and placed it carefully in a crevice in the rocks, above the high tide line, where it would wait.

### The Turning Light

Then she stood up, brushed the sand from her knees, and began the walk up the cliff path to the lighthouse. There were 142 steps to climb, and the lens needed cleaning, and sunset was only twelve hours away.

The starfish glowed softly as she passed, their light following her like a trail of small blue lanterns, steady and faithful, asking for nothing but the hum of the turning light.

*The End*
